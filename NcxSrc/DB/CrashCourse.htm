Appendix 5: A Crash Course in Greek Translation

Greek was a favorite among philosophers, because they could say many different things, on many different levels, with the exact same phrase.  Have you ever wondered if that might have been the reason for the Gospels being written in Greek?

This puts you out about $15 or less, depending on your choices.

The first hurdle you have to get past on actually learning Greek is its Alphabet, and then the dreaded word-endings; those nasty things called tenses, genders, plurality, cases, voices, and moods.  Like most Indo-European languages, the ancient Koine Greek of the New Covenant is highly inflective.  For example, nouns have five cases (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative, and vocative), three genders (masculine, feminine, and neuter), and three numbers.  Verbs have five moods (indicative, imperative, infinitive, subjective, and optative), three voices (active, middle, and passive), as well as three persons, plus other various forms.  Indeed, you can break it down even more, by simply looking at the last 1 to 3 characters at the end of a word (some few are up to 5) and tell if they are singular, plural, possessive, and so on (and you thought you were through with that after you got out of school).

 

The origin of the Greek language has many theories. One suggests that it came with a migration of proto-Greek speakers into Greece, which is dated to any period between BCE 3200 to 1900.  Another assumes that Greek evolved within Greece itself, out of an earlier Indo-European language.

The first known Greek script was the Linear B Syllabary, used for the archaic Mycenaean dialect, which was not deciphered until 1953.  After the fall of the Mycenaean civilization, there was a period of about five hundred years when writing was not used. Greek has always been written in the Greek alphabet, which is assumed to be derived from Phoenician (Egyptian Canaanite).  This was at about the time of Homer.  There is a single obscure reference in Homer's poetry suggesting that he might have even been aware of writing.

Attic Greek was the language of Athens, and most surviving classical Greek literature is written in this.  Alexander the Great was instrumental in combining the various Greek dialects, to form Koine Greek, which means “common” Greek.  It is often called New Testament Greek due to its most famous literary work.  These merged dialects allowed Alexander’s combined army to communicate between each other, and was subsequently taught to all the inhabitants of the regions that he conquered, turning it into a Universal Language, much like English is today.  The Greek language evolved during the Hellenistic period, and for many centuries it was the “Lingua Franca” of the Roman Empire.  From this came the Greek that was the official language of the Eastern Roman Empire (the Byzantine Empire) and to become the Modern Greek of today.  Modern Greek has a more conservative form, called Katharevousa, which includes numerous Ancient Greek words pronounced in a modern way, and the spoken form Dhimotiki, which since 1976, is the official language of Greece, instead of Katharevousa.  Modern Greek has less tense rules, dropping dative and vocative tenses.

 

What I am about to cover should be in no way considered complete, but it will give you a “taste” of Greek, and the tables I provide will be all that most will ever need; for plurality and gender.

Accents

As you have seen in the above examples, under the “Greek Name” heading, all Greek words have one vowel that has an accent mark on it (much like one syllable in English being stressed).  As one would expect, this indicates a stress mark, giving it emphasis.  Notice that Koine Greek did not contain stress marks, let alone any type of punctuation, whatsoever, until the 9th century.

 

Letters with Similar Sounds

Just as in English, Greek has several letters that produce the same sound, though, unlike English, which is actually a melting pot of languages (Like Dutch is with English, French, and German), Greek does not go crazy with this.  They have only three sounds:

 

1)     The “i” as in machine is made by the Greek letters h, i, u, ei, oi, and iu.

2)     The “o” as in lord is made by the Greek letters o and w.

3)     The “e” as in pet is made by the Greek letters e and ai.

Letters that Sound Differently by Placement

Some letters, or as you saw from previously, 2-letter combinations, sound differently if they appear at the beginning of a word, as opposed to anywhere else in a word.  This is the part I hate, but because I am normally transcribing on paper, and not trying to talk to someone in Athens, I usually do not worry too much about them.

 

1)                 Greek au and eu sound like AF and EF, respectively, when they appear before letters that sound like P, T, K, F, TH, KH, PS, or KS.  However, they sound like AV and EV, respectively, everywhere else.

2)                 Greek s sounds like Z before letters that sound like B, D, G, Z, M, N, R, V, Y, GH, and (do you not love exceptions?) sometimes L.  It sounds like S everywhere else.

3)                 p, t, and k are equivalent to P, T, and K, respectively, but sound like B, D, and G when these letters are at the beginning of a word, and the preceding word ends in Greek letter n.

4)                 At the beginning of words, the Greek mt sounds like B, but B or MB elsewhere.  Greek nt sounds like D, but D or ND elsewhere.  Greek gk sounds like G, but G or NG elsewhere.

5)                 Vowels, or vowel combinations, like, h, i, u, ei, and oi, all sound like the “I” in machine.

 

Greek Word Order

Like in English, basic Greek word order is sequenced as Subject, Verb, and Object.  However, unlike English, Greek word order is much more flexible.  What does this mean?  It means you can toss the subject, verb, and object around in their order, and they will still mean the same thing.  For example, the phrase “(the) Peter has come” can be expressed as both “O PetroV hrqe”, as well as “Hrqe o PetroV”.  Note that “o” (Ho) can often mean “the, this, that, these, etc.”  Refer to the table at the top of the next page regarding this common “Ho” word.

Special Translated Word Combinations

Often Koine Greek will use one or more words to express what we today will convey with only a single word.  Most of them are very easy to spot.  A few that confuse some people are:

 

·        “And But”.  This can of course remain thus, or it can be further transliterated to “Therefore”, “Furthermore”, or “Forthwith”.  This is used copiously in the New Covenant Greek text.

·        “But And”.  This would be read much like “Likewise”.

·        “That The”.  As in “…on that the day...”   Here we would simply read the “The” as “Very”, or as “Exact”, or as “Specific”, making this phrase “…on that very day…”  See Acts 8:1 for an example.

·        “But us”, and similar phrasing.  This is used as in “These sayings but us are shameful”.  This would be read in the sense of, “These sayings said of us are shameful”, or “These saying said are shameful to us”.  See Luke 11:45 for an example.

·        “The But”.  This should be read in the sense of making a specifically important point.  You might use this in a lecture, beginning with “Therefore” or “Hence”.  Romans 13:9 is a good example.

 

Putting Greek Word Definitions under the Microscope

The problem with Koine Greek is that often translators did not look very hard at the definitions for words before choosing out, or even inventing a “synonym” to use.  Worse, often the definitions for words were later redefined.  This problem is legion.  For example, I recently noticed that there is still a note by some single-minded theologian under the term Christ, which implies that this traditional royal title was “owned” exclusively by Jesus, which is untrue, as this title had been passed on from regal father to first-born regal son since the time of King David.  Indeed, Joseph, Jesus’ father, was also a Davidic King; hence, he was also a Christ.

 

The most disturbing problem you will face is willful mistranslation, where the word is very straightforward, but a cited example of doing this, which could have actually been anything, is instead used as the translation.  Many such words leave me scratching my head.

For example, in Luke 18:38, some poor man, filled with misery, is bewailing: “But he cried, saying, Jesus, Offspring of David, mercy to me.”  In the next verse, he was quickly told to keep the peace: “And they which went before rebuked him, that he should hold his peace.”  The Greek word used to rebuke him is “epetimwn”.  This is translated in the KJV (and all others), variously as “rebuke”, “charge”, and “straightly charge”.   However, the reason I am scratching my head, is its definition:

 

eprtimwn    (epitimaw)  Epitimao     (ep-ee-tee-mah'-o)

Verb

 

1. To show honour to, to honour.

2. To raise the price of.

3. To adjudge, award, in the sense of merited penalty.

4. To tax with fault, rate, chide, rebuke, reprove, censure severely.

    A. To admonish or charge sharply.

 

Notice the first word on the top row is how it is used within the actual verse.  Next to it, in parentheses, is its root word.  Next to that is the “Latinized” version of the Greek root, and then followed by its pronunciation guide.  Below that is the word usage, such as Verb, Noun, Adjective, etc.  Last is the definition and how it is, or can be used.  I have yet to fleece all the centuries-later-added redefinitions of words.

Of four definitions given, it regards elevations, raising, and honoring, and indeed to show honor (be careful with the third citation, because a penalty was not just bad news).  Somehow it acquired a fourth, very much darker definition, of lowering, rebuking, and admonishing, and rather severely.  If it were left to me, I would simply drop definition 4 entirely.  The ancient mind in Judea was much more civil than it might be depicted in the cinema.  Beggars and the infirm were treated with utmost respect.  This being the case, it is likely that this poor man was not rebuked, but rather treated with respect and honor, and simply asked to keep the peace (be calm; which the word “oiwpaw”, translated to “keep the peace”, also means).  This is actually one of those verses that, in reading the Greek on your own, do not quite say what you read in your Bibles (the New Covenant is loaded with these; Acts and Romans will quite literally lift you out of your chair).

 

In considering such redefinitions, one has to also wonder how the idea of an “Inn” was ever invented for the Gospel period, and used in the Good News of Luke (see Luke 2:7), because “Inns” did not exist in Judea.  The word for “common room” was mistranslated to “Inn”, which simply meant that there was no room in the common room of the house they were staying in to put a feeding bin; the standard baby cradle for all of Judea, so they had to place it elsewhere; likely in a private room.  The popular “stable” did not exist in Judea, except at Roman Garrisons, and sheep and donkeys and goats were kept in wall-less overhead coverings or lean-tos (according to Matthew, Jesus was born in a house, and the idea of a son of God being born in a stable was introduced from Persian Mithra theology).  Also, it was the duty of every Jewish citizen to keep their doors open and a place at their tables open for travelers, inviting any passing them by in as welcomed guests (it was considered good luck to get such a wandering boarder, not to mention feeding the needy), as is common to Jewish Charity (meaning Love).

 

Still another example is the many references to sheep and lambs, such as the Greek word “erpeton”.  This word is actually used for any small four-legged animal, and even for birds, but it would mean more so goats (not sheep) in the majority of cases, and sometimes cattle (until after the Middle Ages, cattle were no bigger than Great Danes), though again, it is never specific.  Indeed, it is my understanding that there is a version of the New Covenant for some South Pacific islands where this word is translated to pigs; because that is the only such small creature they specifically were familiar with.  Some people have condemned the translators for this, but the word is actually general enough that such an interpretation can in fact be valid.  For example, if eating sheep was thought to be so common, then where did the “Sabbatical Goat” come from, and why was it not called a “Sabbatical Lamb”?  Trivia: Though many may associate the term “Sabbatical Goat” with witchcraft, it actually comes from Judaism.  A Sabbatical was the ancient, original form of the Sabbath, taken by mystics, such as those following Chapter 6 of the Book of Numbers, governing the Nazarite Order.  While in ascetic seclusion, they drank no wine or ate meat.  When they took their Sabbatical (“going out unto the world”), they shaved their long hair and beard, and feasted on a goat, or some other small four-legged animal or bird that would fall under the general “erpeton” term.

 

Yet another favorite example is the fact that the New Covenant seems to be filled with references to “eat”; they sat down to meat, they ate meat, they were fed meat, etc.  Even though the people of Judea were by no means vegetarians, the plain fact was that meat was an expensive commodity, and was reserved for celebrations and such.  Further, you are going to find that all (well, maybe a couple might slip by, such as Jesus eating broiled fish) references to “meat” actually simply reference food, or more specifically, to unleavened bread, which was the main course at dining tables.  In fairness, this is not to say that they sometimes did in fact eat meat, but there is no sure way of knowing.  However, with an understanding of its great expense and scarcity, chances are strongly in favor of it being bread.  Just because it is simply stated as food, we cannot really assume anything, though bread with honeycomb, goat milk, and sometimes fish was the more common fare.

 

Another example that I had covered previously in this book is worth re-examining.  This regards the Disciple Thomas, who was also known as Prince Philip Herod I (referred to as simply Philip by Herod Antipas; see Matthew 14:3), father of Salome, and former husband of his and Antipas’ half-sister, Herodias.  This was how he got the Thomas (Twin) pseudonym; for he was likened to Esau, twin brother to Jacob, losing his right of inheritance when his mother, Miriamme II, divorced his father, Herod the Great, thereby losing princely peerage and seniority rights to his half-brother, Antipas.

In John 20:25, he has been famously portrayed as a doubter.  However, being a devoted member of the Hebrew faction, who, along with their leader, John the Baptist, was dead-set against Antipas, and like the Pharisees, he believed ardently in reincarnation and the perpetuation of the spirit.  Indeed, the only thing Thomas ever really had doubts about was the really gross idea of making dead, rotting flesh again alive.  In any case, if you were to look to the Greek text of John 20:25, you would see that he is in fact stating that regardless of seeing these things of Jesus or not, he will believe.  Actually, the word “believe” is more so a conviction of understanding, as most people really do not fully comprehend the real meaning of the word “believe”; which is not actually based on blind faith, but rather a conviction through understanding.

 

Following is the Greek text for John 20:25:

 

elegon oun autw oi alloi maqhtai ewrakamen ton kurion o de eipen autoiV ean mh idw en taiV cersin autou ton tupon twn hlwn kai balw ton daktulon mou eiV ton tupon twn hlwn kai balw mou thn ceira eiV thn pleuran autou ou mh pisteusw

 

Now, here is the traditional translation from the KJV:

 

The other disciples therefore said unto him, We have seen the Lord. But he said unto them, Except I shall see in his hands the print of the nails, and put my finger into the print of the nails, and thrust my hand into his side, I will not believe.

 

Finally, here is the direct translation of that same verse taken directly from the Greek:

 

Telling therefore to him these other disciples, We saw the Master. This then said he, Even if I do not see on those hand of him the marks of the nails, and cast of these fingers of me unto those marks of the nails, and cast of me of this hand unto the side of him, I will not not comprehend.

 

What he is saying here is that regardless of whether or not he sees these things or feels these wounds, he WILL believe, emphasizing his ardent belief regardless of these things by using “I will not not comprehend”, rather than an ordinary acknowledgement of “I will comprehend.” (Note that there is a very strong debate going on right now over John 20:27 regarding the words of Jesus to Thomas and this presumed doubt, that they were not in fact early Church fabrications that simply made it into the verified Greek texts).

Notice also the use of the word “Thrust” in the KJV.  You may notice that I instead used a translation of “Cast”.  Why?  First, this really strange redefinition of the Greek word “Ballo” to “Thrust” is only used in regard to Thomas, and nowhere else in the entire New Covenant in all its 121 forms and uses.  Indeed, “Thrust” is an improper word and is an inaccurate translation.  When casting with the hands, one is placing or laying them, not driving them into something like some Bruce Lee or Jackie Chan movie, especially when you note that the Greek word “ballo” is used.  Indeed, the actual definition for this word is, “To throw or let go of a thing without caring where it falls”, or “To scatter”, which is much more accurately translated to “Cast”, “Put”, “Place”, or even better, “Regardless”.  How they arrived at “Thrust” out of this is beyond me.  Indeed, “ballo” is used only “To put into” when voting (Balloting) by placing small pebbles into an urn; called Stoning, or Casting Stones, or as Throwing Stones (though this last oft-used translation is technically inaccurate).  Besides, regarding Lazarus the Leper in Luke 16:20, it states that he was “laid at his gate”, but it actually translates that he was “caste at his gate.”  Are we to assume, in using the Thomas example, that someone, thrusting, had slam-dunked him into his gate?

Further, notice that I used the word “Unto” instead of “Into”.  Why?  Because the Greek word “eiV” means “Among, Unto, To, or Toward”.  You could only use the word “In” or “Into” when it is reflected in a sentence that makes clear the understanding that one is placing something among or toward something, but not inside of it.  If this meaning is not clear, then the use of “In” or “Into” is entirely improper.  This is especially important when you read all over the place about people believing “in” this or that or someone.  In every instance that I have encountered, the word used is most-often the Greek word “en”, and more properly translates to “with” or “along with”, rather than “in” (it really does not help that this Greek word is actually pronounced “en”).

 

A Sample Translation

A quick translation, word-for-word, or “in the rough”, I came up with, was: “Hearing but raising lifelessness, they certainly jeered; they but said to hear thee concerning this but anew. In this manner Paul went away from midst them. Certain but men clave to him believing, among who also Dionysius the Areopagite, and woman named Damaris, and others with them”.  Now, all you need to do is just transliterate it to a “conversational” English form.

Notice that, as with synonyms in any language, you have a number of choices for words to select.  The only things we have to watch out for are words beyond assumptive words like “I will”, “I”, “you have”, etc., but broader, more concise words that are inserted that “were not there”, such as “by them of old time” in Matthew 5:27, which I had found purely at random while writing Appendix 5.  When you see such devices, you can be sure that there was some “tweaking” done to the traditional translation.

Have fun with your first attempt at translation from Greek, and do not depend on the traditional translations.  Explore your own, because, unlike some who will warn against this, Greek was designed to be multiply interpreted; that is its nature and design.  Besides, if you have 12 different monks locked in a room and translating a single Greek verse, chances are good that you will get 12 differing translations of that verse as a result.

This ability of the laity finally being able to interpret the books of the Bible is something that many of those in ecclesiastic power have admonished and even ordered people not to do.  It is not that it is or was ever sinful; they simply did not want you to find out what was truly there.  Besides, if it was so sinful, then why is it that they can get away with not simply interpreting it themselves, but actually changing the copies of the source text to suit their own agendas?  Consider this: once the books of the New Covenant canon were collected together in the 4th century under Emperor Constantine, who ensured that the approved books were those that were also compatible with his own worship of his Sun God, Sol Invictus, who he claimed to be the Earthly incarnation of, his official decree then commanded than not one more change would be allowed.  Nevertheless, they soon after “tweaked” over 400 additional pages.

 

One last reminder, which had been expounded upon earlier in this book, but is worth repeating, when you begin performing your own translations:

 

·        Punctuation did not exist at all in Greek until the 9th century, and even then, punctuation consisted of a single “dot” between the lines.  More “modern-ish” marks of punctuation existed only in Vulgar Latin texts and some cursive (scripted, multi-cased) Greek texts, but also introduced in the 9th century.

·        Chapters were not used until the 6th century, with only two samples found from the 5th century.  Chapters were simply made where there was an evident break in the sense of the text.

·        Verses did not exist at all until they were first introduced in 1551 as a means to reference smaller portions of text.

 

What this means is that sentences did not always break where it was assumed, and what we now know as verses did not always break up a sentence with a period, a comma, and such, because this punctuation did not exist.  Often separations between modern verses actually broke up what was previously a single flow of words, in a single thought, not meant to be broken up by breathers, such as commas, or other punctuation.

One example of this was very apparent in the example I had presented much earlier in this book; Acts 8:1 thru 2.  This has been traditionally translated to:

 

“And Saul was consenting unto his death. And at that time there was a great persecution against the church which was at Jerusalem; and they were all scattered abroad throughout the regions of Judaea and Samaria, except the Apostles.  And devout men carried Stephen to his burial, and made great lamentation over him.”

 

This is translated directly from Greek to:

 

“Saul moreover pleased the murder him to become also in/on that the day persecution great against the assembly/church/circle that in Jerusalems all now scattered throughout regions of Judea and Samaria nevertheless the Apostles to collect/carry then/also/but the crowned men pious and/also made lamentation great upon him.”

 

My own English transliteration of this direct translation was:

 

“Saul was moreover pleased with the murder of him, becoming also on that very day a great persecution against the Circle that was in Qumrân, all now scattered abroad throughout regions of Judea and Samaria.  Nevertheless the Apostles then collected the Crowned. Pious men also made a great lamentation regarding him.”
