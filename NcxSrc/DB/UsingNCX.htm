Introduction.

First, an explanation of why I use the term Covenant rather than Testament.   Covenant is simply a more accurate translation of the Aramaic word for “Agreement” than testament.  Of course, simply using the word agreement is a little too vague.

 

This program, New Covenant Bible Greek Translator, is one of the easiest Apostolic New Covenant Bible translator software packages available, accomplishing what some, sometimes-expensive commercial packages offer, and most-often offering easier use and sometimes better functionality (or, at the very least, less complicated).

 

All you have to do is start the program up, choose a book and chapter from the list on the left of the screen, and begin viewing straight translations taken directly from the original Greek text.

 

NOTE: All items marked (New) are new since Version 1.0.  However, most of these new features are indeed exclusively new to Version 2 (most Version 1 enhancements are also greatly expanded).

 

Bible Greek is called Koine (Common), developed by order of Alexander the Great, as a means by which all his armies from all across his vast empire could effectively communicate.  This resulted in this Koine Greek becoming the first truly man-made universal language on the planet, sub sequentially being used by scribes to write books for the broadest possible audience.  Indeed, most Latin terms we use most-often have a Greek origin, going right back to the Koine rendition of Greek (to include the phrases “speaking in like coin”, or, “returning the favor in coin”, naming just two examples of many that actually use the actual language moniker, itself).  The beauty of this language is that with a single phrase, you can say many things on many different levels of interpretation.  The development of philosophy in Greece was no accident; it was a direct result of their language, inviting, for the very first time in easy context, plays on words.  This is much like taking a pseudonym for popular actors and calling them “Stars”.  Entertainment investors are referred to as “Angels”.  Hence, the phrase “The Angels talked to the Stars” has two different meanings; one literal, and one in slang or jargon.  Though people today use slang all the time, few realize that the Jews of Judea used slang just as much, especially those who spoke Greek.  Most people of ancient Judea spoke at least “passable” Greek, but their “common” language was Aramaic.  Contrary to popular belief, Hebrew was spoken almost exclusively by the Priest class, or those who read heavily from the Mosaic Law, though by the Gospel period, many were referring instead to the Greek Septuagint Bible, which was a Greek version of the Hebrew Bible written for Diaspora Jews (and is often cited as actually being more accurate than most Hebrew Bibles).  Indeed, the Book of Hebrews was written for such priestly Diaspora members in Asia Minor.  Trivia: The majority of Jews actually lived outside of Judea, and spoke Greek, not Aramaic or Hebrew.

Why Translate At All? A Practical Demonstration.

I have been often asked why I (or anyone else) should bother to give people the capability to translate from Greek to English when we already have English Translations.  That is easy to answer: because you are going to find that, the English Translations we presently have do not always say what is actually there.  And that is an understatement!

 

For example, most King James Version Only advocates may have no clue how dated the language this beloved version of the Bible actually is; even with the Modern King James Version being released (I will demonstrate shortly – not to mention that it is often less accurate).  Further, before someone else who might be using the New Revised Standard Version, New International Version, the Good News Bible, or some other “modern” rendition feels a bit superior from reading that statement, they themselves are going to discover that their own translations are little better.  Why?  Because they most-often use terms that were “borrowed” directly from the KJV.  And that is the focus of a really big problem in Bible translation: translators, trying not to draw ridicule upon them selves from the “club”; other senior tenured scholars who are nestled comfortably on their laurels, who will often strike like vipers if their “superior” opinions of translation are called into question (they not realizing that such self-conscious attacks only exposes the hill of sand they rest upon).  Frankly, anyone who is that insecure and shortsighted is a fool, and so I have no problem with exposing them (their attacks mean absolutely nothing to me).

 

Would you like a demonstration?  Simply use the New Covenant Bible Greek Translator on chapter 1 of Acts.  You will barely get started and you are going to find the Direct Translation calling those pious scholarly opinions into serious question.

 

For one thing, as you peruse through the whole of the Greek text of the New Covenant, you are going to find out that Jesus is much more all-inclusive, non-sexist, deeper, and a much greater and more amazing person than the “surface” stories we read in “traditional” translations reveal.  We will also find that Paul is exposed as a rather dark character, derisively vicious to those he often wrongfully persecutes, sometimes acting like a bestial man, often inciting riots, and not to mention that his own teachings too often go directly counter to the actual teachings of Jesus reported in the Gospels. You do not normally see these images so clearly from such traditional translations.  But lifting them straight from the Greek text; they are undeniable.  Consider only one single peripheral point: Paul was very vocal about his Roman citizenship (which his father, not he, had earned).  It was a law that all Roman citizens, no matter what religion, were required to worship Caesar as God (and no, there was not a special dispensation for Jews insofar as Roman citizenship was concerned; though there was a dispensation advocated by Caesar Augustus and Julius Caesar that allowed Jews in Judea not to worship Caesar if they were not also Roman citizens; but even this was sharply waning by the time of Tiberius in 14 AD).  If a citizen did not prove worship of Caesar, they could be put to death by the sword.  This proof of Caesar-worship had to be openly demonstrated once a year by all claiming such Roman citizenship.  No exceptions.  Now consider Paul’s situation.

 

The King James Version was completed in the year 1611.  It was conceived under Elizabeth I (prompted by the Bishop’s Bible of 1568, which all traces of had disappeared, which is what inspired the conception of the KJV), but the actual work did not begin until after her death (1603), work beginning in January, 1604.  After it was completed in 1611, there were some few revisions, but they all were to correct word order and misspelled words (these books were of course type-set by hand, inviting lots of errors; even today with powerful word-processing computers our books still contain lots of errors), not to mention that word spelling itself had changed dramatically even by half way through the 17th century.  The biggest problem we are now facing is not so much that the KJV is as wrong as some people claim (hard to deny when even my application provides you with almost 7000 corrections to errors in the KJV), but rather most of these are “errors” of semantics – word meanings have simply changed significantly over time.  Indeed, though you might think that its Elizabethan English is perfectly understandable, I may be stepping on some toes by saying that I strongly doubt your confidence (unless you are a scholar who has specialized in the language of Elizabethan England), and I will now proceed to prove it.

Text Navigation in the New Covenant Bible Greek Translator.

You can use the mouse to click on a word in either the displayed Greek text, or the direct translation below it.  When each word is clicked, a list of synonyms (variants) is offered so that you can “fine-tune” its transliteration into English.  Also as an aid, a generous description of the Greek word root is offered, helping you better understand exactly what the original authors wanted to convey to their readers.  Additionally, notes are quite offered on each verse being displayed, regarding translation notes and corrections to the “traditional” translations, listing about 7000 corrections to errors in older bible text (1300 more were added since Version 1 of this software).

 

Keyboard Navigation features make scanning through verses, synonyms, chapters, and book a breeze.  See Word Navigation, below.

 

 

Word Navigation.

Using Left Arrow and Right Arrow allows you to scan through the Greek words (and reflected in the Direct Translation) from one end of a verse to the other.

 

Using Up Arrow and Down Arrow allows you to navigate through Synonyms for each Greek word to adjust the Direct Translation’s representation of that word in the translated text.

 

Using Home and End keys to move to the beginning or the end of a verse.

 

Verse, Chapter, and Book Navigation.

Using Ctrl-Left Arrow and Ctrl-Right Arrow allows you to move between consecutive verses.  Notice that when the limits of a chapter are breached, it will process the adjoining chapter.  Likewise, when books are breached, the next consecutive book is then processed.  Hence, when you go below verse 1 chapter 5, the last verse of chapter 4 is displayed.  Likewise, when you go below the first verse of the first chapter of a book, the last verse of the previous chapter of the previous book is displayed.  Conversely, advancing belong the end of a chapter advances to the next chapter, and advancing beyond the end of a book advances to the next book.  If the extreme ends of the New Covenant are breached, they wrap around to the extreme opposite end (Revelation 22:21 will wrap around to Matthew 1:1, or vice versa, for example).

 

Using Ctrl-Up Arrow or Ctrl-Down Arrow allows you to navigate through consecutive chapters.  When you navigate in this way, the first verse of the next consecutive chapter is selected.

 

Using Alt-Left Arrow or Alt-Up Arrow backs up one book, and using Alt-Right Arrow and Alt-Down Arrow advances to the next book in the list.  When you advance in this way, the first verse of the first chapter of the selected book is displayed.

 

 

Using the Slider and Report Field for Verse Navigation.

You can also navigate through verses within a chapter by clicking on the slider bar at the bottom of the Greek Text panel, noted to the left, to increment one verse at a time, or simply slide the button on the slider by pointing at it, holding the selection button on the mouse down, and dragging it left or right.

 

You can click on the verse report field (the one reporting “Verse 3 of 25” in this example), and a dialog box will pop up.

 

Here, you can type in the desired verse in the chapter.

 

 

Using the “GO TO” option.

Finally, a new feature is that you can press the F5 key, or choose  the “Go to book chapter:verse” option from the file menu.  This allows you to type a book, chapter and verse very quickly and advance to that location.

 

 

An addition to Version 2.1.3 is the three dropdown lists in the toolbar, where you can select the Book, Chapter, and Verse with no typing. When a location other than the current active is selected, the “GO” button is enabled.  Selecting it goes directly to the new selection.

 

Using the History Back and Forward Buttons

Finally, two new buttons added allow you to navigate through a “history” list if verses you have visited during the current session, using a “Back” and “Next” button found below the “Copy All” button, much like navigating in a Browser Window.  Notice that these are initially disabled, until you begin moving through verses, whereupon the “back” (left arrow) button will enable, allowing you to go back through the history list.  Once you go back, you can then proceed forward through the list with the right arrow button.

 

 

With Version 2.1.3, you can also view the session history list by clicking the magnifying glass button between the history buttons.  From this list, you can also select a verse from the history to jump directly to.  The list is displayed in the order that they were viewed, though you can also choose to list them in alphabetical or biblical order.  The current verse is initially highlighted in the list.

 

Additional features allow you to remove older duplicates in the list, remove selected verses from the list, and save not only the list from the current session, but to optionally save the list, between sessions, of up to the last 1000 viewed entries.

 

If you move the mouse pointer over a verse in the list, the contents of the verse is displayed using the currently active Bible version.

Creating Your Own Personalized Version of the Bible.

From the File menu, select the Create Personal Bible option (This caption has been changed to “Initialize a personal version of the Bible…” in version 2.1.3).  You are then presented with a choice of which Bible version to base your version upon (this allows you to have some text before you have finished transliterating your full version.

 

Once, you have your own version created, an additional edit window is displayed at the bottom left of the form which allows you to edit a direct translation, properly transliterating it into English.

 

With this window, you can transliterate the verse from scratch; select the “Copy Xlt” button to copy the text of the direct translation (without brackets).  You can then fine-tune the text by adding punctuation, capitalization, plurality/gender fixes, and such.  Be aware that the Greek text does not contain punctuation.  Punctuation in Greek text did not exist before the ninth century, and even then, punctuation consisted of only a single dot between lines of text (verses themselves did not exist until 1551).  Do not worry about traditional punctuation.  Your punctuation will most likely be just as accurate as anyone’s will.

 

Indeed, often “verses” have split a single unbroken sentence in two.  Take for example, Acts 8:1 and 8:2.  This is actually breaking a sentence in two, and people have since tried to see them as two separate entities.  See the direct translations for your self to see what I mean (this example is elaborated on near the end of this help file).

 

You can also select the “Copy MPV” button (My Personal Version) and this will copy the verse from your personal version to the edit window.  When you are satisfied, you can select the “Update MPV” button to save this update to your personal version.  When you have done this, when a verse is displayed that you have manually translated, it will be shown in the verse field with White text on a Navy Blue (Dark Blue) background.

 

 

 

Extract KJV Words and Translate Modernly (New).

Instead of constantly referring to the King James Version dictionary in an attempt to translate from King James English to Modern English (see View King James Version Dictionary), you can instead enable the “Extract KJV words and translate modernly” option under the new View menu, and words or phrases that match those found in the KJV dictionary are marked in the verse within square brackets.  Below the verse, if KJV word matches are found, the words are listed, along with their modern definitions.  Be acutely aware that the modern definitions, though accurate for 17th century usage, might not actually express their original, 1st century definition for the Greek words.  Interestingly, you might be quite surprised to discover that people in the 17th century actually had a much better understanding of what the New Covenant actually said, than we do today, because their understanding of the words were at that time correct for their lexicon, as opposed to how those words are modernly translated.  For example, they would have known that “Communed” actually meant what we think of as “Reasoned”, or that “Leasing” for them meant the same as “Lying” is for us, today.

 

 

Adding Personal Notes to Verses (New).

In addition to the Theological notes for verses, you can add you own personal notes regarding a verse.  The data you add will be appended to the displayed theological notes, but stored separately in a MyNotes.txt database file.

 

To save any changes, simple close the form by clicking the mouse on the “X” button in the upper-right corner of the form.  To cancel any changes, select the CANCEL button, or press the ESC key.

 

Notice that when personal notes are present, a note appears above the Theological notes area that informs you of this.  By clicking the mouse of this note field, it will bring your personal notes to the top of the box, and highlights the “My Personal Notes:” heading. This is especially handy when there are a lot of Theological notes for the verse, and you want to see what your notes are.

 

Use the Green Arrow buttons in the toolbar to move to the previous or the next Personal Note defined within your database, or select the “View Next Personal Note” or “View Previous Personal Note” options in the View Menu.

 

 

View All Personal Notes (New).

You can view all of the personal notes you have defined in the Definitions panel (which you can afterward save via the COPY button and paste them into a word processor).   Select this new option from the View menu.  Choose options as you require.  If you do not choose to display a book heading, the Book name will be included in the Chapter Report line.  If you do not choose to report a Chapter heading, then the Chapter and Verse will be added above each verse data.  As you can see, you can mix and match the various options.  For example, leaving all fields unchecked will report the Book, chapter and verse in the format “Book, Chap:Verse;”, and this will be followed by the personal notes.  Personally, I like to have as much information as possible, and prefer the above settings.

 

View Personal Notes for Only This Chapter (New).

If you would like to view only the personal Notes that you have made for the current chapter, you can select the “View Personal Notes for Only This Chapter” option from the View menu.  This will display each verse that has personal notes attached to it, plus the notes you have made regarding it.  Again, this are displayed in the Definitions panel in the upper-right quadrant of the window.

 

 

View Theological Notes for This Chapter (New).

Synonym Selection.

Synonyms are similar words that can have the same meaning.  English is a very rich language, containing well over 30 millions different instances of word usage (3 million words alone have been added between 1963 and 1983; mostly techno-jargon).  Were synonyms not permitted, our lexicon would be very much smaller, and poetry would not exist (even so, our average daily lexicon is about 5000 different words – still more than some entire languages).  Also, often, Greek words use the same word to mean many different things, based upon how they are used in the sentence.  As such, it is advisable to review the notes displayed in the upper right panel for word usage.

 

As you progress through the bible, you might notice that sometimes the word used looks little like its base word.  We will explore this in a moment.  Here, however, we are concerned with trying to convey in English what the author of the text had intended.  Granted, were you a native speaker of Koine Greek, this might not be much of a problem, but languages in 2 millennia have changed sometimes dramatically; word order is often more specific, and how word combinations change meaning of a phrase.  For example, a transliteration of “But Peter walked in the hot sun” could have been expressed in Greek in the following order and still be considered valid: “This Peter but walked the sun hot under.”

 

Further, certain word combinations convey a different meaning.  For example, “John and but said” would be read as “John therefore said”.  But of course, these are still easy to transliterate.  Besides, the direct translation is also understandable.

The presented lists of synonyms are by no means complete.  For proper translation, you WILL need to often add new synonyms (for example, a synonym of “to walk” may need to have a “walking” synonym added to smooth the flow of the direct translation text, or adding “of” to a number of words).  By referencing the notes on the Greek word in the upper-right panel, you may notice that a definition that is presented in the definition, and would more accurately convey the sense of the text, can be added to the list and will be stored in your list of English synonyms for that Greek word.

This allows you to add a word to the synonym list.  Suppose you want to add a valid alternative of “Upon” to the above list for “In” (actually, this particular word most-often only means “in” in the sense of being “with” or “at” something), we would select the “Add” button.  Notice that whatever word is currently highlighted is displayed in a dialog as a “template”.  This is useful with many words that you simply want to render “to walk” to “walked” or “walking”.  When you select the OK button, the new word is selected as the new default, and the direct translation is updated to reflect it.  If you accidentally enter a word that is already in the list, it will not be added, but the matched word will be selected and updated in the direct translation field.

How Synonyms are Selected.

Most synonyms in each list were initially added in alphabetical order.  When you first begin using the program, there are no “user-selected” words applied to that verse for the direct translation window.  By default, the first word in each list is used.  When you change an entry, that new selection is locked in and recorded for being always used for that specific word for that specific verse (until you change it again).  The selection also becomes the “new” default entry for subsequent words in other verses that you have not yet gone through.  For example, the default in the above example was “among”.  By changing it to “In”, that word in that specific verse will always use “In”, even if in another verse you select “Among”.  However, because you chose “In”, in subsequent verses that use the same Greek word which you have not edited (the verses that you have already fine-tuned will not be altered, so do not panic), but in new verses (yet unedited verses), the word be displayed with the “In” as the new “default” word, until you in turn change it again (the program actually stores a reference map, WordMap.txt, for every single individual user-selected word in the New Covenant).  Hence, though editing words in a verse will lock in your particular selections for each of those words in that edited verse; in the background, each selected word becomes the new “favorite” for that Greek word when it is encountered in unedited verses.  An unedited verse is simply a verse that you have not yet viewed, or rather, you are viewing for the first time; once a verse is viewed within the program, the selections for word for that verse are “soft-locked” in for that verse thereafter, until you manually change them.  I realize that this sounds a bit complicated, but it is really something that you do not really need to worry about.  Once you start using the program, you will see what I mean.  Needless to say, verses that you have worked on or have already viewed will not later change when you set a new general English synonym as a “current default” word for a Greek word.

Plurality.

This program does some really primitive plurality checking.  Instead of digging through charts and the like, the application simply first checks to see if the word is more than three characters long.  The methodology, which I also do mentally, is as follows:

·        The following words are known to be singular: “h”, “to”, “ton”, “th”, “thn”, “tou”, “thV”, “enaV”, “ena”, “enan”, “mia”, “mian”, “miaV”, “enoV”.  Notice that the oft-used “o” can be singular or plural, but I treat it as singular for the sake of simplicity.  Just keep this little exception in mind.

Notes on Greek Inflection and Sentence Structure.

Like many ancient languages, Koine Greek is very inflective (Modern Greek is as well, but much less so).  Hence, the word you select may sometimes not look like the actual word it is based upon (called a “root” word).  This is because of cases, plurality, tenses, voicing, etc.  Most often, you will notice the word ending is different, or even that a letter or even several have been added to its beginning.  These are more complicated that our more modern and efficient languages, but we can still easily demonstrate this with a word like “Understand”.  We can modify this with prefixing or suffixing variants to “tighten” the meaning of the root word, creating such words as “Misunderstand”, “Understands”, “Understanding”, and “Misunderstanding”, to name only a few.  For example, you may notice the letters “x” and “k” and “c” and “ss” and “kk” swapping around, or, “e”, “a”, or “i” inserted between consonants that are not in the base word (or removed from the base word), or some new prefix, such as “e”, “ete”, or “eke”, other many others (not to mention suffixes, sometimes very long ones), so do not be surprised to see rendering “exaggeilhte” from a root of “kale”.  Further, there is the twist that Greek has two lowercase characters for the letter “s”, being “s” and “V”, which sometimes seem to swap (legally) in some texts at random (for example, many newer Greek text that end in “s” will use “s”, but most older Greek texts which will use “s” for all letters within a word, but will use “V” at the end when “s” is used to terminate the word.  On top of all this, sometimes it looks like endings were added in odd jumbled assortments that look like they were picked out simply by the roll of dice (the term “it is all Greek to me” was not just a joke).
